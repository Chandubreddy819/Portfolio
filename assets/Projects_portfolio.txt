Hardware Design for a Low-Power BLE &
Mioty Localization Tag
● Project Timeline: August 2025 – Ongoing
● Project Type: University Project (M.Sc. ICT, FAU Erlangen-Nuremberg)
● Technologies: KiCad, STM32F103, Semtech SX1280, TDK ICM-20948, Low-Power

Project Goal
As the hardware lead on a three-person team, I am responsible for designing a new, compact, and
power-efficient localization tag. The project's objective is to replace a module-based prototype with a
custom PCB that integrates discrete components, reducing both size and cost while enabling operation
on a single coin cell.
My Contributions
● Component-Level PCB Design: I designed a new schematic and multi-layer PCB in KiCad,
integrating the Semtech SX1280 LoRa transceiver, STM32F103CBU6 microcontroller, and a
TDK ICM-20948 9-axis motion sensor (IMU).
● Low-Power Optimization: The entire circuit is engineered for ultra-low power consumption
from a single CR2032 coin cell, based on manufacturer application notes and rigorous power
budget analysis.
● RF Antenna Implementation: I am implementing a 2.4 GHz inverted-F antenna based on a
Texas Instruments reference design (SWRA351b) to ensure optimal wireless performance.

Key Engineering Challenge & Solution: Power Budget
The primary design constraint was ensuring long-term operation on a CR2032 coin cell, which has a
limited pulse current capability.
● Problem: Analysis showed that MIOTY transmissions require a ~24 mA pulse current,
exceeding the ~15-20 mA peak rating of a standard CR2032 cell, which would cause voltage
sag and system instability.
● Analysis: I conducted a detailed current consumption analysis for each component in all
operational states (TX, Sleep, BLE Advertising). The calculated average current draw in the
target low-power mode is ~38 μA.
● Result & Solution: This analysis predicts a theoretical battery life of approximately 8.1
months. To resolve the pulse current issue, I specified a low-ESR reservoir capacitor (~10
mF) to supplement the coin cell during high-demand transmission bursts, ensuring a stable
power rail.

Project: Automation of Sensor Testing &
Validation for Powertrain Solutions
● Company: Robert Bosch
● Role: Graduate Apprentice
● Timeline: August 2022 – August 2023
● Technologies: Python (NumPy, Matplotlib, PyQt), MATLAB, Raspberry Pi, CAN/SENT,
JIRA, Simulink
Project Goal
The manual validation process for automotive powertrain sensors was a significant bottleneck, being
slow, costly, and susceptible to inconsistencies. My primary objective was to design and implement an
automated system to improve the efficiency, reliability, and cost-effectiveness of these essential tests.
My Contributions & Responsibilities
● Automated Test System Design: I designed and validated custom sensor test control units
using Raspberry Pi and various microcontrollers. These units were responsible for interfacing
with sensors and acquiring measurement data.
● Scripting & Data Analysis: I developed a suite of automated measurement and analysis
scripts using Python and MATLAB. These scripts managed the entire test cycle: from
acquiring raw CAN/SENT sensor signals to processing the data and generating final plots and
reports.
● Root-Cause Failure Analysis: I led investigations into signal integrity issues such as signal
clipping, drift, and noise. My process involved using spectral analysis and creating
reproducible test scripts to systematically identify the root cause of hardware and wiring
faults.
● Process & Documentation: I was responsible for the calibration of test benches and probes,
and I documented all procedures and results to ensure full traceability for product release
readiness.
